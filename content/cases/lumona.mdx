## Overview

Lumona is an ERP & POS concept I drove as product manager. The idea was simple to say and brutal to execute: give retail businesses one place to run their whole supply chain — procurement, cataloging, assortment, stock, and point-of-sale — without needing a consultant to set it up.

It's industry-agnostic by design. Whether you're moving fashion, F&B, or hardware, the underlying flow is the same: stuff comes in, gets organized, gets sold. Lumona models that flow once and adapts the surface.

<Callout icon="Lightbulb">
  My job here wasn't to write the code — it was to make a genuinely complex domain feel obvious. Most of the work was deciding what *not* to build.
</Callout>

> **Note:** Shipped as an MVP — this case focuses on the product thinking and system design behind it. No vanity metrics here, just the work.

## What I owned

As PM/PO I sat between the business, design, and engineering and was accountable for *what* we built and *why*:

- Mapped the end-to-end supply chain flow and turned it into a product spec
- Defined the core modules and, more importantly, their boundaries
- Prioritized ruthlessly — what's v1, what's "nice to have," what's a trap
- Built the information architecture and prototyped key flows in Figma
- Wrote the user stories and acceptance criteria the team built against

---

## The hard part: supply chain is a web, not a list

Retail ops people don't think in tidy screens. A single purchase order touches suppliers, items, warehouses, costs, and tax — and changing one ripples into all the others. The challenge was modeling those relationships so the product felt powerful to a warehouse manager but didn't scare off a small shop owner.

### Core modules

- **Procurement** — purchase orders, supplier management, receiving
- **Item Cataloging** — a single source of truth for every product, variant, and unit
- **Assortment** — deciding what's sold where, and keeping it consistent across channels
- **Point of Sale** — the front-of-house that has to *just work*, offline-friendly and fast

---

## Product decisions I'm proud of

1. **Catalog-first architecture.** Everything hangs off one clean item model, so procurement, assortment, and POS all speak the same language instead of duplicating data.
2. **Progressive complexity.** A new user sees a simple POS. Power users unlock procurement and assortment when they're ready — the depth is there, but it's opt-in.
3. **Killing features on purpose.** The fastest way to make an ERP unusable is to ship every request. I held the line on scope so v1 could actually ship.

## Where it stands

Lumona shipped as an MVP, and it's still evolving. The thinking holds up: it's the piece I point to when I want to show how I approach a messy domain — understand the real workflow, model it once, and design the surface so complexity is something you grow into, not something that hits you on day one.
